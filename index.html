Education and Positions

I'm a Ph.D. student of International Relations at Graduate School of Law, Kyoto University. Also, I was a Doctoral Research Fellow (DC1) at Japan Society for the Promotion of Science (JSPS) from 2015 to 2018 and a Visiting Student Research Collaborator (VSRC) at Department of Politics, Princeton University in 2016. I completed my M.A. in political science and LL.B. at the Department of Law, Kyoto University in 2015 and 2013, respectively.

Research Interests

I study the causes of peace and war, forcusing on mutual interactions between economy and security. Specifically, I’m interested in (1) how economic interdependence among nations affects their security and (2) how states chooses foreign economic policy given these security implications of economic ties. In addition, to capture international politico-economic dynamics in broader context, I’m going to study international poitical economy and international institutions, especially in Asia. I primarily employ game-theoretic approach, especially dynamic (Markov) game. I also use quantitative methods, case studies, text analysis and survey experiments.

Analyzing International Developmental Loan Markets with Rival Lenders
Abstract
Despite the deepening of interdependence, international institutions are becoming increasingly fragmentary and unable to coordinate states’ policy choices. Such a coordination failure is serious in the realm of developmental finance, where major states vie against one another in extending loan contracts to developing states through separate institutions. Competitive lending is a double-edged sword: on the one hand, it may satisfy strong credit demands for infrastructure improvement in the developing world, while, on the other, it may also generate excessive lending and borrowing, increasing the risks of repayment difficulty and even sovereign debt default. Using a model of common agency, we analyze (1) how two lenders issue loan contracts with a single borrower to induce the foreign policy behaviors they prefer and (2) how the borrower determines its foreign policy to maximize loans. The analysis hypothesizes that, when the borrower assumes foreign policy neutralism, the lenders issue greater loan contracts than what they believe are optimal and that this excess persists as long as the lenders refuse to delegate the lending tasks to a common international financial institution that can sort out their varying preferences and provide common-agency control. We test this hypothesis against quantitative data pertaining to Cold War experiences and provide qualitative evidence on American and Soviet foreign economic policies. Our analytical results have implications for historical and contemporary Asian political economy.
Climate Policy Diffusion: Interdependence through Economic or Normative Considerations?
Abstract
Climate policy implementation under the Paris Agreement seems to rely on reciprocity and the related social norms connecting countries. In contrast, analysts of interdependent climate policy have sought to explain the interdependence of policy implementation based mainly on economic considerations. To fill the gap between the two, we seek to evaluate directions and channels associated with policy influence from other countries in reference to both economic and normative considerations. Analytically, drawing on the policy diffusion literature, we develop an empirical model to comprehend the multiplicity of policy influence: directions and channels. The multiplicity was only partially examined by the previous studies, which focused on positive and negative complement (e.g. race to the top and bottom) related to economic activities. Our empirical results instead uncover a pattern of substitutive behavior, or free riding characterized as policy deterioration in response to improvement by peers as well as altruism characterized as improvement in response to deterioration. The former ensues through income competition driven by economic considerations, while the latter through regional ties embracing normative considerations. These results provide an important implication for innovative global climate policy management.
